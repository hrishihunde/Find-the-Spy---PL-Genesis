## 1. Civilian Displacement

Despite the strike targeting military installations exclusively, the psychological impact on civilian populations triggered significant displacement within Israel's southern region. The Home Front Command ordered the activation of civil defence protocols for all communities within 40 kilometres of the Negev impact sites. Approximately 18,000 residents of communities near Sdot Micha Air Base, Tze'elim, and surrounding Bedouin townships self-evacuated northward within the first 12 hours.

The Israeli Home Front Command opened 14 emergency shelters in Be'er Sheva, Arad, and Dimona, accommodating approximately 6,200 evacuees. The remaining displaced individuals stayed with family or in private accommodations. By 48 hours post-strike, approximately 60% of evacuees had returned home, though residents of communities closest to the Sdot Micha impact site reported reluctance to return pending additional Iron Dome deployments.

The Bedouin population of the Negev — approximately 270,000 people living in both recognised and unrecognised villages — faced particular challenges. Many unrecognised Bedouin villages lack access to public shelters and early warning systems. The Association for Civil Rights in Israel (ACRI) reported that residents of at least 8 unrecognised villages received no Home Front Command alert notifications and were not included in the shelter allocation plan. These communities relied on word-of-mouth warnings from neighbours and self-organised evacuation to Be'er Sheva.

## 2. Psychological Impact

The Ministry of Health's mental health division reported a 280% increase in calls to the national psychological emergency hotline (ERAN) in the 48 hours following the strike. The most common reported symptoms were acute anxiety, sleep disturbance, and hypervigilance — particularly among residents who had experienced previous rocket attacks during the 2023 conflict.

Children were disproportionately affected. The Israel Trauma Coalition reported that 34% of callers mentioned children's distress as a primary concern. Schools in the Negev region were closed for 3 days following the strike, affecting approximately 42,000 students. When schools reopened, the Ministry of Education deployed 120 additional school counsellors to the region.

In Iran, reliable data on civilian psychological impact is limited. Iranian state media reported "national unity and resolve" in response to the operation, but independent sources suggest anxiety about potential Israeli retaliation was widespread, particularly in western Iranian cities within range of Israeli long-range strike capabilities.

## 3. ICRC and International Humanitarian Response

The International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) issued a statement on March 14 reminding all parties of their obligations under international humanitarian law (IHL), specifically the principles of distinction (between military and civilian targets), proportionality (in the use of force relative to military advantage), and precaution (in avoiding civilian harm). The ICRC noted that while the strike appeared to target military installations, "the humanitarian consequences of any escalation would be devastating for civilian populations across the region."

The ICRC's delegation in Israel conducted rapid assessments in affected communities within 24 hours. Their preliminary findings indicated no direct civilian casualties but documented "significant displacement and acute psychological distress among communities adjacent to impact sites." The ICRC also noted concerns about unexploded ordnance (UXO) from drone and missile fragments scattered across the Negev, estimating that debris was spread over approximately 120 square kilometres.

Magen David Adom (Israel's national Red Cross/Red Crescent society) activated its mass casualty protocol within minutes of the strike. While mass casualties did not materialise, the activation mobilised 2,400 additional volunteers and 180 ambulances to southern Israel. MDA reported treating 7 military personnel (as per IDF figures) and an additional 23 civilians for anxiety-related symptoms, minor injuries from shelter entry, and pre-existing conditions exacerbated by stress.

## 4. UNHCR and Refugee Implications

The UNHCR expressed concern about the potential impact on refugee populations in the broader region. Syria hosts approximately 6.8 million internally displaced persons, and the launch of cruise missiles from Syrian territory raises questions about the safety of displacement camps in southern Syria (Daraa governorate), where several of the Soumar launch sites were located.

UNHCR's Damascus office reported no direct impact on refugee camps or settlements but noted that "the use of Syrian territory as a launch platform for attacks on a third country further undermines the already fragile security environment for displaced populations." The agency called on all parties to "ensure that humanitarian spaces are not instrumentalised for military purposes."

Jordan, which hosts approximately 660,000 registered Syrian refugees and shares a border with both Israel and Syria, raised its military alert level along the northern and western borders. UNHCR Jordan reported no refugee displacement related to the strike but noted increased anxiety among refugee communities in the Zaatari and Azraq camps.

## 5. Medical Infrastructure and Preparedness

Israeli hospitals in the southern region activated their emergency protocols. Soroka Medical Centre in Be'er Sheva — the Negev's primary trauma centre — moved to Level 3 emergency status, cancelling elective surgeries and recalling off-duty staff. The hospital treated the 7 injured military personnel and managed surge demand from anxiety-related civilian presentations.

The WHO's Eastern Mediterranean Regional Office (EMRO) issued a situation report on March 15 noting that "health systems in the region are already under strain from multiple concurrent crises, including the ongoing conflict in Gaza, the Syrian civil war, and the Yemeni humanitarian emergency." The WHO expressed concern that a broader Iran-Israel escalation would further overwhelm health infrastructure in neighbouring countries.

## 6. Humanitarian Law Compliance Assessment

Preliminary analysis by human rights organisations suggests that the Iranian strike was largely compliant with the principle of distinction — all confirmed impact sites were military installations, and no civilian infrastructure was intentionally targeted. However, questions remain about precaution: the launch of 147 slow-moving drones over populated areas of Iraq, Jordan, and the West Bank created risks for civilian populations along the flight path. At least 3 drone fragments were recovered in populated areas of eastern Jordan, though no injuries were reported.

Human Rights Watch (HRW) stated that it would conduct a full investigation into the strike's compliance with IHL, including an assessment of the flight path risks. Amnesty International called for "independent investigations into whether adequate precautionary measures were taken to minimise risks to civilian populations in transit countries."

The question of Israeli retaliation looms large for humanitarian organisations. OCHA (UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs) issued a contingency planning directive to all UN agencies in the region, outlining scenarios for potential escalation and their humanitarian implications — ranging from a limited Israeli counter-strike (estimated 50,000 additional displaced persons) to a broader regional conflict involving Hezbollah and Iraqi militias (estimated 500,000 to 1.2 million additional displaced persons).
