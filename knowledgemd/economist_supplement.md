## 1. Oil Market Reaction

The immediate market reaction to Operation Righteous Dawn was sharp but partially self-correcting. Brent crude futures opened 14.7% higher in Asian trading on March 14, reaching an intraday peak of $101.20 per barrel before settling at $97.40 by the London close — an 11.3% gain on the day. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) followed a similar trajectory, closing at $93.85, up 10.1%.

The initial spike reflected the geopolitical risk premium that traders assign to any direct military exchange between Iran and Israel. The partial correction occurred after Iran's Foreign Minister Bagheri signalled that "the matter is concluded," reducing fears of sustained conflict. Options market data showed a significant increase in demand for call options on Brent at strike prices above $100, indicating that traders were hedging against further escalation rather than pricing in a permanent supply disruption.

By the end of the first trading week (March 14–18), Brent had settled at $94.60 — a 6.8% net increase from pre-strike levels. This residual premium reflects ongoing uncertainty rather than actual supply disruption.

## 2. Strait of Hormuz Risk Assessment

The Strait of Hormuz is the single most critical chokepoint in the global energy supply chain. Approximately 21 million barrels of oil per day — roughly 21% of global petroleum consumption — transit the Strait. Any sustained closure would constitute the most significant supply disruption since the 1973 oil embargo.

Following the March 14 strike, the Strait remained open with no reported disruption to commercial shipping. Iran's navy conducted what it described as "routine patrols" in the Strait area but did not interfere with tanker traffic. Lloyd's of London and the Joint War Committee assessed the situation as elevated risk but not a blockade threat, adding the Eastern Mediterranean (but not the Persian Gulf) to the Listed Areas designation for war risk insurance.

Maritime war risk premiums for Persian Gulf transits increased by approximately 0.15 percentage points, adding an estimated $30,000–50,000 per voyage for a standard VLCC (Very Large Crude Carrier). This cost is ultimately passed through to refiners and consumers.

The US Fifth Fleet maintained an enhanced presence in the Strait area, with at least two guided-missile destroyers conducting freedom-of-navigation operations. CENTCOM issued a NOTAM (Notice to Airmen) and NOTMAR (Notice to Mariners) advising commercial operators of "heightened military activity" in the region but did not impose any transit restrictions.

## 3. OPEC+ Response

OPEC+ held an extraordinary virtual ministerial meeting on March 16, 2026. The cartel issued a statement expressing "concern over the security situation" but announced no immediate changes to production quotas. The current OPEC+ production agreement, extended through the end of 2026, includes voluntary cuts of approximately 2.2 million barrels per day by key members (Saudi Arabia: 1 million bpd voluntary cut; Russia: 500,000 bpd; UAE, Iraq, Kuwait: combined 700,000 bpd).

Saudi Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman stated that OPEC+ "stands ready to take appropriate measures to ensure market stability" — standard language that does not commit to either increasing or maintaining current production levels. Analysts interpreted this as a signal that Saudi Arabia would not immediately release spare capacity to offset the geopolitical premium, effectively allowing prices to remain elevated.

Iran's oil exports — currently approximately 1.5 million barrels per day, mostly to China under sanction-evasion mechanisms — were not immediately affected by the strike. However, the prospect of tightened enforcement of existing US sanctions on Iranian oil could reduce these flows by an estimated 300,000–500,000 bpd over the next 3 to 6 months, according to energy consultancy Rystad Energy.

## 4. Financial Market Spillovers

Beyond crude oil, the strike triggered measurable effects across multiple asset classes:

**Natural Gas:** European TTF natural gas futures rose 4.2% on March 14, reflecting concerns about potential disruption to Eastern Mediterranean gas infrastructure — specifically the Leviathan and Tamar fields offshore Israel, which supply gas to Egypt and Jordan. No actual disruption to gas production or export occurred.

**Equities:** The Tel Aviv Stock Exchange (TASE) TA-35 index fell 3.1% on March 14 before recovering 1.2% the following day. The Tehran Stock Exchange (TSE) TEDPIX index fell 2.4%, reflecting concerns about intensified sanctions. European indices (STOXX 600) dipped 0.8%, and the S&P 500 opened 0.6% lower before recovering to close nearly flat.

**Currency:** The Israeli shekel weakened 1.8% against the US dollar in the first 24 hours, the largest single-day move since October 2023. The Iranian rial, already under severe pressure from sanctions, declined a further 3.2% on the unofficial market. Safe-haven flows pushed the Swiss franc up 0.4% and gold futures up 2.1% to $2,340 per ounce.

**Credit Default Swaps:** Israel's 5-year CDS spread widened by 12 basis points to 87 bps — elevated but well below the 130+ bps reached during previous escalations. Iran does not have a liquid CDS market due to sanctions.

## 5. Trade Route Disruption

The strike did not directly disrupt major trade routes, but it compounded existing concerns about Red Sea shipping disruptions. Houthi attacks on commercial shipping in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden, ongoing since late 2023, had already diverted approximately 60% of container traffic from the Suez Canal to the Cape of Good Hope route, adding 10–14 days to Asia-Europe transit times and increasing shipping costs by approximately $1,500–2,500 per twenty-foot equivalent unit (TEU).

The Iran-Israel escalation raised concerns that Houthi operations might intensify in solidarity with Iran, further pressuring Red Sea routes. Insurance premiums for Red Sea transits increased by an additional 0.1 percentage points following the strike, on top of the already-elevated war risk premiums.

## 6. Macroeconomic Implications

The IMF's Middle East and Central Asia Department issued a brief assessment on March 17, noting that "a sustained oil price increase of $10 per barrel above baseline would reduce global GDP growth by approximately 0.15 percentage points and increase global headline inflation by approximately 0.4 percentage points." With Brent approximately $6–7 above pre-strike baseline, the estimated impact is a 0.10 percentage point drag on global growth and a 0.25–0.30 percentage point increase in inflation.

For the directly affected economies: Israel's GDP growth forecast for 2026 was revised downward by 0.3 percentage points (from 3.2% to 2.9%) by Bank of Israel economists, reflecting defence spending increases and reduced consumer confidence. Iran's economy, already constrained by sanctions, faces an estimated additional 0.5 percentage point GDP contraction if sanctions enforcement tightens as expected.
