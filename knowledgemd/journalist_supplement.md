## 1. Wire Service Chronology

The first wire report was filed by Agence France-Presse (AFP) at 02:23 UTC, eight minutes after the initial launches were detected. The AFP report, sourced from a stringer in Baghdad, described "multiple bright objects crossing Iraqi airspace heading west." Reuters followed at 02:31 UTC with a report citing Iranian state television's live broadcast of the IRGC's operational announcement. The Associated Press filed its first bulletin at 02:38 UTC, sourced from the IDF spokesperson's initial confirmation of "a large-scale aerial threat from the east."

By 03:00 UTC, all three major wire services had confirmed the nature of the attack — a combined drone, ballistic missile, and cruise missile operation. However, initial reports diverged significantly on scale. AFP initially reported "over 200 projectiles," Reuters cited "approximately 150," and the AP reported "at least 100 aerial objects." The IDF's official count of 197 total projectiles was not confirmed until 09:45 UTC, nearly seven hours after the strike began.

## 2. Source Verification Challenges

Journalists covering the event faced significant source verification challenges in the first 12 hours. Iranian state media (IRNA, Press TV, Fars News) reported dramatically inflated damage figures, claiming the destruction of "an underground weapons depot near Dimona" and "at least three F-35 aircraft." These claims were amplified by Iran-aligned media outlets across the region, including Al-Mayadeen (Lebanon) and Al-Alam (Arabic-language Iranian outlet).

Israeli military censorship (the IDF Censor) initially restricted reporting on specific impact locations, damage details, and casualty figures. This created a 6-hour information vacuum in which unverified claims circulated widely. Israeli media outlets (Haaretz, Ynet, Channel 12) were permitted to report on the interception operation but not on specific damage until the IDF released its official assessment at 09:45 UTC.

Open-source intelligence (OSINT) analysts partially filled this gap. Accounts on social media platforms posted geolocated videos of interceptions over the Negev, and commercial satellite tasking by Planet Labs and Maxar Technologies produced the first independent imagery within 14 hours. However, the OSINT community also generated several false positives — including a widely-shared claim of a missile impact near the Dimona nuclear facility, which satellite imagery later disproved.

## 3. Press Conference Analysis

The IDF held its first on-the-record press conference at 12:00 UTC on March 14. IDF Spokesperson Rear Admiral Daniel Hagari presented a slide deck showing the interception timeline, a map of impact points, and photographs of recovered drone fragments. Hagari described the interception rate as "approximately 89%" and characterised the damage as "limited and non-strategic." He declined to answer questions about whether Israel planned retaliatory strikes, citing "operational security."

Iran's IRGC spokesperson held a televised briefing at 10:00 UTC, claiming that "all primary objectives were struck with precision" and that "the Zionist regime's so-called Iron Dome failed catastrophically." The briefing included footage from IRGC drone cameras, though analysts noted the footage appeared to show only launch sequences, not impact verification. No satellite or ground-level damage imagery was presented by Iranian sources.

The US Department of Defense held a background briefing at 14:00 UTC. A senior defence official, speaking on condition of anonymity, confirmed US naval participation in the intercept operation and stated that "the US-Israel defensive partnership performed as designed." The official declined to specify the number of US intercepts, stating only that "several ballistic missiles were engaged by Aegis-equipped vessels."

## 4. Media Coverage Patterns

Analysis of the first 48 hours of global media coverage reveals three distinct narrative clusters. Western outlets (AP, Reuters, BBC, CNN, NYT) focused primarily on the interception success, the US defensive role, and the risk of regional escalation. Middle Eastern outlets split along predictable lines: Gulf-aligned media (Al Arabiya, The National) condemned the Iranian strike, while Iran-aligned outlets (Al-Mayadeen, Press TV) amplified claims of Israeli damage and framed the strike as justified retaliation.

Non-aligned outlets (Al Jazeera, TRT World, NDTV) adopted a more analytical framing, emphasising the cycle of escalation and the absence of a diplomatic offramp. Al Jazeera's coverage was notable for its balanced sourcing, citing both IDF and IRGC statements while flagging unverified claims from both sides.

Social media platforms saw a surge in engagement. On X (formerly Twitter), the hashtag #IranAttack trended globally for 18 hours. Meta reported a 340% increase in traffic to Iran- and Israel-related news content on Facebook and Instagram. TikTok saw a wave of short-form videos mixing verified interception footage with unverified claims, prompting the platform to deploy additional fact-checking labels within 8 hours.

## 5. Journalist Safety and Access

Three international journalists were briefly detained by Israeli security forces near the Sdot Micha perimeter in the hours after the strike. They were released without charges after credential verification. Reporters Without Borders (RSF) issued a statement calling for "unimpeded press access to strike sites" and criticising the IDF's initial reporting restrictions.

In Iran, at least two journalists from reformist outlets were summoned by the Ministry of Intelligence for "consultations" after publishing articles questioning the accuracy of IRGC damage claims. The Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ) documented these cases and called for their protection.

## 6. Fact-Checking Ecosystem Response

Major fact-checking organisations published assessments within 24 hours. The International Fact-Checking Network (IFCN) coordinated a rapid-response effort involving 14 member organisations across 9 countries. Key debunked claims included: the alleged Dimona impact (disproved by satellite imagery), a viral video purportedly showing an Israeli F-35 being shot down (identified as footage from a 2019 video game), and a claim that 47 Israeli soldiers were killed (no evidence; IDF reported 7 injuries, none fatal).

The speed of the fact-checking response was notable — faster than in previous Iran-Israel escalations — but still lagged behind the initial spread of false claims by an estimated 4 to 8 hours.
