## 1. Incident Overview

On March 14, 2026, at approximately 02:15 UTC, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched a coordinated strike against Israeli military installations in the Negev desert. The operation, designated "Operation Righteous Dawn" by Iranian state media, involved 147 Shahed-136 one-way attack drones, 32 Shahab-3 medium-range ballistic missiles, and 18 Soumar ground-launched cruise missiles. The strike package was launched from three staging areas: western Iran (Kermanshah province), eastern Iraq (via IRGC proxy positions), and southern Syria (Daraa governorate).

Israeli air defences intercepted approximately 89% of the incoming projectiles. The Iron Dome system neutralised 94 of the 147 drones. David's Sling intercepted 28 of the 32 ballistic missiles. The Arrow-3 exoatmospheric interceptor engaged 4 high-trajectory Shahab-3 variants at altitudes exceeding 100 kilometres. Of the cruise missiles, 14 of 18 were intercepted by a combination of Iron Dome and IAF F-35I Adir fighter jets scrambled from Nevatim Air Base.

Despite the high interception rate, 11 projectiles reached their targets. Two Shahab-3 missiles struck the perimeter of the Sdot Micha Air Base, damaging a maintenance hangar and a runway taxiway. Three Shahed-136 drones impacted the Ramon Airfield, destroying two ground support vehicles and damaging a radar installation. Six additional drones struck open desert areas within the Tze'elim military training complex, causing no structural damage but triggering secondary fires in scrubland.

## 2. Casualties and Damage Assessment

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) reported 7 military personnel injured, none critically. All injuries occurred at Sdot Micha Air Base from shrapnel and debris. No civilian casualties were reported. The IDF classified the overall damage as "operationally limited" — no combat aircraft were destroyed, no command infrastructure was compromised, and all affected installations were operational within 36 hours.

Iran claimed 22 Israeli military targets were destroyed, including "a key nuclear-capable aircraft shelter." This claim has not been corroborated by any independent satellite imagery or third-party assessment. Commercial satellite imagery from Planet Labs, captured 14 hours after the strike, confirmed damage consistent with the IDF's published assessment.

## 3. United States Response

Within 90 minutes of the first missile launch detection, the United States Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed the deployment of the USS Gerald R. Ford carrier strike group from its position in the central Mediterranean to the Eastern Mediterranean, approximately 180 nautical miles west of the Israeli coast. The carrier strike group includes the guided-missile cruiser USS Normandy, destroyers USS Thomas Hudner and USS Ramage, and an estimated 75 aircraft aboard the Ford.

President Mitchell issued a statement at 06:30 UTC from the White House Situation Room, stating: "The United States stands unequivocally with Israel's right to defend itself. We are in close coordination with our Israeli counterparts and are providing real-time intelligence and defensive support." The statement stopped short of announcing direct US military action against Iran.

The Pentagon confirmed that US Aegis-equipped destroyers in the Eastern Mediterranean participated in the defensive intercept operation, engaging at least 6 incoming ballistic missiles with SM-3 Block IIA interceptors. This marks the second confirmed instance of US naval assets directly intercepting Iranian projectiles targeting Israel.

## 4. International Reactions

The United Nations Security Council convened an emergency session within 4 hours of the strike. The session, chaired by the rotating presidency (Brazil), produced no binding resolution. Russia and China vetoed a Western-drafted resolution condemning the Iranian strike. Russia's ambassador described the attack as "a predictable consequence of sustained provocations." China's representative called for "maximum restraint from all parties."

The European Union issued a joint statement through High Representative for Foreign Affairs calling the Iranian strike "a dangerous escalation that risks wider regional conflict." The EU stopped short of announcing new sanctions but stated that "all options remain on the table."

The Arab League convened an extraordinary session in Cairo. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Bahrain issued a joint communiqué condemning the strike. Jordan recalled its ambassador from Tehran for consultations. Egypt called for "immediate de-escalation and a return to diplomatic channels."

Turkey's position was notably measured. President Erdogan called the situation "deeply concerning" but did not condemn Iran directly, instead calling for "an international conference to address root causes."

## 5. Iranian Justification

Iran's Supreme National Security Council issued a formal statement describing the strike as "a legitimate act of self-defence under Article 51 of the United Nations Charter, in response to the assassination of IRGC Quds Force deputy commander Brigadier General Hossein Mohammadi in Damascus on February 28, 2026." Iran attributed the assassination to Israeli intelligence services, a claim Israel neither confirmed nor denied.

Iranian Foreign Minister Javad Bagheri stated in a televised address that "the matter is concluded from Iran's perspective" and that "no further military action is planned unless Iran or its interests are targeted again." This language closely mirrors Iran's public messaging after previous retaliatory strikes.

The US Fifth Fleet in Bahrain raised its force protection condition to FPCON Charlie. US bases in Iraq and Syria reported no incoming fire in the 72-hour window following the Iranian strike, though CENTCOM noted "elevated threat posture from Iran-aligned militia groups."
