## 1. Historical Precedent: Iran-Israel Direct Confrontation

Operation Righteous Dawn represents the third instance of direct Iranian military strikes against Israeli territory, following the April 2024 strike (Operation True Promise) and the October 2024 follow-up. Each escalation has followed a consistent pattern: an Israeli targeted killing of a senior IRGC or Hezbollah figure, followed by an Iranian retaliatory strike calibrated to demonstrate capability while limiting escalation risk.

The April 2024 precedent is the most instructive comparator. That operation involved approximately 170 drones, 30 cruise missiles, and 120 ballistic missiles — a larger total projectile count (approximately 320) than the March 2026 strike (197). The April 2024 strike achieved a lower impact rate: Israeli, US, UK, French, and Jordanian assets intercepted approximately 99% of incoming projectiles. The March 2026 strike's 89% interception rate — lower than the 2024 figure — reflects Iran's adaptation: fewer but more capable projectiles (particularly the Soumar cruise missiles, not used in 2024) and the absence of coalition intercept support from the UK, France, and Jordan in 2026.

The escalation pattern suggests a learning cycle on both sides. Iran has progressively refined its strike packages to stress Israeli defences — moving from quantity (2024) toward a more balanced mix of speed, altitude, and flight profile diversity (2026). Israel's layered defence has proven robust but faces an increasingly complex threat environment with each iteration.

## 2. Deterrence Theory Application

The Iran-Israel exchange fits within the framework of Thomas Schelling's concept of "the diplomacy of violence" — the use of military force not to achieve battlefield victory but to communicate resolve and establish boundaries. Iran's strikes are calibrated to fall within what strategists call the "proportionality window": forceful enough to demonstrate capability and satisfy domestic audiences, limited enough to avoid triggering a full-scale Israeli or US military response.

This dynamic is consistent with Robert Jervis's "spiral model" of international conflict, in which each retaliatory action raises the baseline of acceptable violence, making subsequent escalation more likely. The progression from the 2024 to the 2026 strike — with increasing sophistication and reduced coalition intercept support — illustrates the spiral in action.

However, the exchange also exhibits elements of "stability-instability paradox" (Glenn Snyder, 1965). The mutual vulnerability created by Iran's ballistic missile capability and Israel's nuclear deterrent (neither confirmed nor denied by Israel) creates stability at the strategic level — neither side is willing to risk a full-scale war — while simultaneously permitting instability at lower levels of conflict. Targeted assassinations and retaliatory strikes become "acceptable" precisely because both sides believe the other will not escalate to the strategic level.

## 3. Proxy Network Dynamics

The March 2026 strike occurred in a significantly altered proxy landscape compared to 2024. Hezbollah's military capacity in Lebanon was substantially degraded following the Israel-Hezbollah conflict of late 2024, which resulted in the destruction of an estimated 60–70% of Hezbollah's pre-war rocket and missile arsenal. The absence of Hezbollah participation in the March 2026 strike — in contrast to coordinated Hezbollah rocket attacks during the April 2024 event — reflects this degraded capacity.

Iran's proxy network in Iraq remains operational. The Islamic Resistance in Iraq (a coalition of Iran-aligned militias including Kata'ib Hezbollah and Harakat Hezbollah al-Nujaba) had conducted over 170 attacks on US forces in Iraq and Syria between October 2023 and March 2026. However, these groups did not conduct coordinated strikes in support of Operation Righteous Dawn — a notable absence that may reflect Iranian command decisions to contain the escalation scope.

The Houthi movement in Yemen — the most operationally active Iranian proxy in 2024–2025 — continued its Red Sea shipping disruption campaign but did not launch direct attacks on Israel during the March 2026 window. This suggests Iranian compartmentalisation: keeping proxy operations on separate escalation timelines to preserve strategic flexibility.

## 4. Nuclear Dimension

The nuclear dimension of the Iran-Israel relationship provides essential context for the March 2026 strike. The JCPOA (Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action), from which the US withdrew in 2018, had constrained Iran's uranium enrichment to 3.67% with limited stockpiles. Following the US withdrawal, Iran progressively increased enrichment — reaching 60% purity by 2023 and maintaining stockpiles of 60%-enriched uranium sufficient, with further enrichment, for approximately 3 nuclear weapons (as estimated by the IAEA in its quarterly reports).

Israel's undeclared nuclear arsenal — estimated by international analysts at 80–90 warheads — provides existential deterrence but also constrains Israeli decision-making. Any Israeli retaliatory strike that threatens the Iranian regime's survival risks triggering an Iranian "breakout" — a rapid sprint to weaponise enriched uranium. This calculus imposes restraint on both sides and helps explain why the cycle of targeted killings and retaliatory strikes has remained below the threshold of full-scale war.

The proximity of the Sdot Micha impacts to Israel's presumed nuclear weapon storage facilities (the Sdot Micha base is associated with Israel's Jericho ballistic missile programme) adds a destabilising dimension. While the impacts were on the base perimeter and did not approach sensitive facilities, the symbolism of Iranian missiles landing near Israel's nuclear infrastructure carries significant escalatory signaling.

## 5. International Relations Theory: Power Transition and Regional Order

The Iran-Israel conflict exists within a broader regional power transition. The Abraham Accords (2020) represented a structural shift in Middle Eastern alignment, creating a nascent Israeli-Gulf security architecture. Iran perceives this alignment as an existential threat — an encirclement strategy designed to isolate it diplomatically and militarily.

The March 2026 strike can be interpreted through the lens of power transition theory (A.F.K. Organski): Iran's strikes are not attempts to defeat Israel militarily but to demonstrate that the emerging regional order cannot guarantee security for its participants. By striking Israel directly, Iran signals to Abraham Accords signatories that normalisation with Israel carries security costs — a message aimed as much at Riyadh and Abu Dhabi as at Jerusalem.

Saudi Arabia's decision to pause normalisation talks following the strike validates this interpretation. Iran's strategic objective may be less about damaging Israeli military infrastructure and more about disrupting the diplomatic architecture that threatens its regional position.

## 6. Escalation Management and Offramps

Historical analysis of similar escalation cycles (India-Pakistan Kargil crisis 1999, US-Iran Strait of Hormuz tensions 2019–2020) suggests that de-escalation requires three conditions: a face-saving narrative for the initiating party, credible back-channel communication, and a neutral mediator acceptable to both sides.

Iran's statement that "the matter is concluded" provides the first condition — a declared endpoint that allows de-escalation without appearing to submit to pressure. The Omani and Qatari mediation efforts provide the second and third conditions, though their effectiveness depends on whether Israel chooses to accept the current situation or pursue a retaliatory cycle.

The key risk factor for further escalation is domestic political pressure in Israel. The Israeli security establishment's traditional doctrine of "imposing costs for every attack" creates pressure for retaliation. If the Israeli government chooses a limited retaliatory strike — targeting IRGC infrastructure in Syria or Iranian proxy assets rather than Iranian sovereign territory — the escalation spiral may be contained. A strike on Iranian territory, however, would likely trigger a new cycle.
